Blood in Eye

TBlood in the eye, also known as hyphema, can be a medical emergency and requires immediate care. It can be caused by trauma to the eye, such as a sports injury, or medical conditions that affect blood, such as leukemia, hemophilia, and diabetes. Hyphema can cause blood to collect behind the cornea and iris


Blood in Eye may be associated with:

  • Black Eye
  • Corneal Ulcer
  • Blepharitis
  • Conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye")
  • Corneal Laceration
  • Hyphema

Burning Eyes

Burning eyes is a stinging or irritating sensation in the eyes that may be caused by many different reasons, including allergies, infections, chemical irritants, and low humidity.


Burning Eyes may be associated with:

  • COVID-19
  • Conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye")
  • Eye Syndrome

Eyelid Redness and Rashes

Redness and rashes around the eyes can have many causes, including skin conditions, infections, and health issues. A type of contact dermatitis called eyelid dermatitis, also known as eyelid eczema, can cause redness, irritation, and swelling of the eyelids. Other symptoms include stinging, burning, itching, and skin that becomes scaly


Eyelid Redness and Rashes may be associated with:

  • Dermatitis
  • Eczema
  • Blepharitis
  • Eye Anemia

Red Eye

Red eyes, or bloodshot eyes, are often caused by swollen or dilated blood vessels on the surface of the eye, which can be due to irritation or disease. This can happen in one or both eyes, and can develop over time or appear suddenly.


Red Eye may be associated with:

  • Conjunctivitis
  • Scleritis
  • Blepharitis
  • Angle-closure glaucoma

Blurry Vision

Blurry vision is when your vision isn't sharp and crisp, and objects appear hazy or out of focus. It can be caused by many things, including eye conditions, medical conditions, or side effects of certain medicines


Blurry Vision may be associated with:

  • Diabetes
  • Stroke
  • Glaucoma
  • Eye Anemia

Blind Spots

A blind spot in the eye, also known as a scotoma, can be caused by damaged retina. A growing blind spot can be a sign of retinal detachment, which occurs when the layers of the retina separate, causing vision loss. Prompt surgery is required to prevent blindness in the eye


Blind Spots may be associated with:

  • Macular degeneration
  • Scotoma
  • Eye Anemia

Flashes and Floaters

Flashes and floaters in the eyes are usually caused by a normal process called posterior vitreous detachment (PVD), where the gel inside the eye changes. This process can be caused by aging, eye injuries, or other diseases or conditions. Flashes occur when the vitreous pulls on the retina, while floaters are caused by tiny clumps of gel or cells inside the vitreous


Flashes and Floaters may be associated with:

  • Retinal tear
  • Retinal detachment
  • Eye Anemia

Temporary Darkening of Vision

Amaurosis fugax refers to a temporary black-out of vision. This is usually affects one eye, is painless, and is often described like a "shade coming down over the vision" of that eye. The black-out may last minutes, and then the vision returns


Temporary Darkening of Vision may be associated with:

  • Retinal detachment
  • Migraines
  • Papilledema
  • Amaurosis fugax
  • Retinal vein occlusion
  • Retinal migraine

Natural Ways to Help Improve Vision and Eye Health


Improving vision and maintaining eye health naturally involves several lifestyle changes and practices. Here are some tips:


  1. Eat a Balanced Diet: Include foods rich in vitamins A, C, and E, as well as minerals like zinc and selenium. These nutrients support eye health. Foods like carrots, spinach, kale, sweet potatoes, citrus fruits, berries, nuts, seeds, and fish are beneficial
  2. Stay Hydrated: Drinking an adequate amount of water helps maintain moisture in the eyes and prevents dryness, which can lead to discomfort and vision problems.
  3. Protect Your Eyes from UV Rays: Wear sunglasses that block harmful UV rays when outdoors. Prolonged exposure to UV radiation can increase the risk of cataracts and macular degeneration.
  4. Take Regular Breaks from Screens: If you spend long hours in front of a computer or digital devices, follow the 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, take a 20-second break and look at something 20 feet away to reduce eye strain.
  5. Practice Eye Exercises: Simple eye exercises can help improve focus, flexibility, and coordination of eye muscles. These exercises include blinking, focusing on near and distant objects, rolling your eyes, and palming (covering your eyes with your palms)
  6. Maintain Proper Lighting: Ensure that your workspace and living areas are well-lit to reduce eye strain. Avoid glare from windows and overhead lights by using curtains, blinds, or anti-glare screens.
  7. Get Regular Exercise: Physical activity improves blood circulation, including to the eyes. Aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate exercise most days of the week.
  8. Quit Smoking: Smoking increases the risk of developing age-related macular degeneration, cataracts, and other eye conditions. Quitting smoking can help preserve eye health
  9. Get Sufficient Sleep: Lack of sleep can lead to eye fatigue and strain. Aim for 7-8 hours of quality sleep each night to allow your eyes to rest and regenerate.
  10. Stay Hydrated: Drinking plenty of water supports overall health, including eye health. Dehydration can contribute to dry eyes and discomfort.
  11. Manage Chronic Conditions: Conditions like diabetes and hypertension can affect eye health. Manage these conditions through proper medication, diet, and lifestyle choices to minimize their impact on vision.
